Analyzing the Most Important Basic Statistic in Winning NBA Basketball Games¶

By Praneeth Malyala¶

In basketball, there are many statistics in the game like points, rebounds, assists, steals, and more that are kept track of. Generally, for each stat, the more each team has, the better. But I want to investigate which of those statistics (not including points obviously) is most conducive to a team winning.

In theory, arguments could be made for the importance of each of those statistics. For instance, rebounds are vital because a defensive rebounding prevents the opposing team from having a 2nd opportunity to score, and an offensive rebound gives the team another opportunity to score, both of which help the team win. Assists are essential because they indicate that the team has good ball movement, which is vital for a team's offense. Steals are imperative because they prevent the opposing team from scoring on that play and oftentimes will give the team an advantageous position to score. Blocks are key because they occur mostly in the paint, stopping the opposing team's easier shots from being scored. As you can see, there is a reasoning for the importance of each statistic for a team winning, but I wish to find out which one statistic is the most critical in leading to a team's win in the NBA. With the help of data science, I hope to find out the answer.

Data Collection¶

To find the most important statistic, I am going to analyze the box scores of NBA regular-season games. To get the data, I downloaded the sqlite file from https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/wyattowalsh/basketball?resource=download, which has box scores from 1946 (when the NBA started) to 2021. From, there I queried the necessary columns from the "GAME" table.

While this may seem counterintuitive as I am getting rid of thousands of observations, the reason is that the game of basketball has changed significantly since the NBA's inception, and to best analyze NBA data, I only want to use the most relevant data to today's game. For example, it was only in 1954 when a 24-second shot clock was introduced, and only in 2001 was illegal defense eliminated and zone defense allowed. Following 2001, changes to NBA regulations have been few and minor, with the majority being about instant replays.

Now that I have handled all missing data, I can proceed to the next step of finding relationships in the data. The big thing to decide was how to approach analyzing the data. For instance, one way I considered was to use categorical variables throughout. I could create a categorical variable of whether the home team's rebounds were greater than the away team's rebounds, and I could repeat this for each statistic like assists, steals, blocks, and so on. The categorical variable would be binary with the choices being greater or less. I could then pair these binary variables with whether the home team wins or not, which I already have in the dataset. From there, I could run a test like a chi-square for each statistic (rebounds, assists, steals...) on whether the home team wins to get correlation results. After that, I can compare p-values to see whether any are significant and compare the chi-square statistic to see which correlates the strongest (If you would like to better undertand how to use a chi-squared test, I recommend reading https://machinelearningmastery.com/chi-squared-test-for-machine-learning/). However, I thought this approach would cause me to lose too much information by categorizing the variables. For example, the home team could have 65 rebounds and the away team could have 20 rebounds, which would be marked as greater. But the home team could also have 22 rebounds, which would still be marked by greater. I wanted to more accurately account for the differences in the game statistics.

As such, the next approach I considered was to make all the variables continuous. For instance, I could create a continuous variable of the difference between the home team's rebounds and the away team's rebounds and could do this for each game statistic (assist, steals... etc). Then, I would create another variable, which would be the difference between the home team's points and the away team's points. Now, I have all the variables I need. I could then run a linear regression between the difference of a game statistic against the difference in points. I could repeat this for each game statistic and can obtain p-values and correlation coefficients, which I can then compare to see how significant the results are. But, I thought that this approach was not entirely what the goal of my project is. I am not trying to find how a game statistic like rebounds can help predict the score but how it contributes to winning. This method was a bit of an overkill and was not my goal for this project.

What I decided on was a combination of both of these approaches. The first approach could not account for the differences in each game statistic, which was a limitation. The second approach could account for the differences but would go too in trying to predict those differences through linear regression. So, I thought the best solution was to create continuous variables of the differences in each game statistic for the home team and away team, but the dependent variable would be a binary variable of whether the home team won or not, which is already in the dataset. I could then use logistic regression to see how well each game statistic correlates with winning games.

Hypothesis Testing¶

The null hypothesis is that each of the game statistics is independent in determing whether a team wins a game. To test this out, I used the logistic regression function from statsmodels with one independent variable at a time. I chose to do one independent variable at a time over using all independent variables together because 'REB_DIF' = 'OREB_DIF' + 'DREB_DIF'. Because of this, the columns will not all be independent of each other and as a result, if I were to run the model together, the p-value for these three values will be 1.00.

Conclusion¶

As you can see in the hypothesis testing section, the p-values in the logistic regression model are very small and all less than 0.05. Therefore, I can reject the null hypothesis that the game statistics are independent in determining whether a team wins a game.

From the graph above, I can deduce which game statistics are the most important in winning games. I see that defensive rebounds have the highest coefficient followed by assists. Surprisingly, both blocks and offensive rebounds had a negative correlation in winning games. Blocks even had the third-largest coefficient of all the game statistics. The negative correlation in offensive rebounds explains why the coefficient for total rebounds was less than that of defensive rebounds. Having negative correlations for turnovers and personal fouls makes sense as teams should try to minimize those. Fewer turnovers mean more possessions on offense, which equates to more chances to score points. Fewer fouls mean the opponents get to shoot fewer free throws, which means a lower score for them.

Right now in the NBA, my favorite team, the Dallas Mavericks, is in the Western Conference Finals and is one series away from playing in the NBA Finals. They are facing the Golden State Warriors who they have faced 4 times during the regular season. Throughout those 4 games, the Mavericks have averaged more offensive rebounds, more defensive rebounds, fewer total rebounds, fewer assists, more blocks, fewer steals, more turnovers, and more fouls. That is 7 out of 8 game statistic correlations favoring the Warriors. So, I am a little bit scared as a Mavericks fan! But, they do average more in the most important statistic in winning games - defensive rebounds, which leads me to the goal of my project. I found that of all the basic game statistics in basketball, earning defensive rebounds is the most conducive to winning games in the NBA.
